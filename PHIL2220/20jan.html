First Meditation
Tue., Jan. 20
In this class, we prepared for discussing the First Meditation, entitled “What can be called into doubt”. We covered the terrain of philosophy and its different branches, where so-called “modern philosophy” fits into the history of Western philosophy, and the basics of the Aristotelian system that tends to characterize the Late Scholasticism that modern philosophers like Descartes are reacting against. Especially important: substances as composites of form and matter, souls as substantial forms of bodies, real definitions as giving the essences of things, the four causes of natural changes, and the extent to which the intellect relies on the imagination and senses. We finished with a quick overview of Anselm’s ontological argument (proving God exists by starting with a definition of God, and showing that existence follows from his essence) and Aquinas’s Five Ways (proving God exists by starting with sensory observations of nature in action and working back to an original divine explanation of those phenomena).
Key Concepts: Certainty, Method of Doubt
