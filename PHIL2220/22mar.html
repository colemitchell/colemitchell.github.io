Hume’s Enquiry
Fri., Mar. 22
In this class, we discuss a chapter from Hume’s A Treatise of Human Nature on personal identity, and a chapter from his Enquiry concerning Human Understanding on free will. Just as Locke was agnostic about the real essence of substances, and Berkeley was adamantly opposed to material substance, Hume is skeptical about the so-called ‘self’ that all our mental perceptions are supposed to inhere in. He offers instead a psychological explanation for why we suppose there to be some sort of personal identity underlying all the mental changes we experience. And as for free will, Hume tries to turn the tables on incompatibilists who think causal necessity is opposed to free will: on the contrary, Hume argues, once free will is rightly understood, human freedom and moral responsibility both depend on a causal necessity that relates our actions to our personality so that our actions are not utterly random.
Key Concepts: Personal Identity, The Self, Free Will, Moral Responsibility
