Ethics: Hume’s sentimentalism
Thu., Apr. 24
In this class, we discuss Hume’s version of sentimentalism: influenced not only by Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, but also Mandeville and Butler. Hume criticizes the idea that reason and the passions can oppose each other, arguing that reason plays an informational role and the passions play a motivational role. This fuels his critique of moral rationalism: reason alone (especially reasoning about relations) is not capable of evaluating anything morally. Moral evaluation requires moral sentiments grounded in the passions. We finish by considering his ‘circle’ argument that justice (i.e., the impartial moral virtue associated with property) is an artificial virtue.
Key Concepts: Reason as Slave of Passions, Motivation Argument against Rationalism, Is-Ought Gap, Natural Virtues and Artificial Virtues
