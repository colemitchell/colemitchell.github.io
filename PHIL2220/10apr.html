Kant’s First Critique
Thu., Apr. 10
In this class, we continue discussing Immanuel Kant’s Critique of Pure Reason, turning to his unique reconciliation of free will and determinism. According to Kant, an observable human action (an action “as appearance”) can be explained in two ways. First, natural necessity: my action can be causally explained by previous events in accordance with natural law, so that it is a deterministically predictable member of a series of appearances in time. Second, freedom: my action can be explained by an underlying ground that is not itself an appearance, my intelligible character outside of time capable of producing empirical effects through practical reason and not subject to the laws of nature. For Kant, all this is a mere unprovable logical possibility; but it does take away the conflict between free will and determinism. Moreover, the principle of ‘ought implies can’ shows our commitment to this possibility in practical life: regardless of a liar’s causal background, they ought not to have lied, which means they were capable of not lying.
Key Concepts: Free Will, Determinism, Causality, Appearance, Thing in Itself, Ought Implies Can
