Hume on moral psychology
Mon., Apr. 22
In this class, we continue discussing Hume’s account of human moral psychology in his Treatise of Human Nature. Hume maintains that reason is incapable of making moral judgments. Again, all reason can do is provide information, and then it is up to our passions to evaluate that information positively or negatively. In particular, Hume critiques the common ‘moral rationalism’ of the early modern period (defended by Cudworth, Malebranche, Leibniz, Bayle, Clarke, Balguy, Wollaston, Wolff, Cockburn, and a bit later Price and Reid), which held that moral truths are eternal truths not even God can change, comparable to mathematics and depending on abstract relations. After critiquing this theory, Hume begins to develop his own version of ‘moral sentimentalism’ (Hutcheson, Hume, Adam Smith), putting emphasis on the non-rational passions built into human nature.
Key Concepts: Moral Evaluation, Moral Rationalism, Moral Sentimentalism
