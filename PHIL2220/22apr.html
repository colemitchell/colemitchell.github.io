Ethics: early sentimentalism
Tue., Apr. 22
In this class, we discuss the early sentimentalism of Lord Shaftesbury (Anthony Ashley Collins, the Third Earl of Shaftesbury) and Francis Hutcheson. In Shaftesbury’s Inquiry concerning Virtue, human virtue is a way of being a good creature (with affections promoting the good of oneself and one’s species) that involves a higher-order affection or sense of right and wrong (comparable to a sense of beauty). For Hutcheson, we have a moral sense (one of our “internal” senses) that generates an idea of approbation when we observe actions stemming from benevolent affections, and neither the moral sense nor the benevolent affections are egoistic. Moreover, Hutcheson argues that rationalist phraseology (e.g., conformity to reason, absolute fitness, relations of things) is all unintelligible unless we presuppose a moral sense for approbation and affections for motivation.
Key Concepts: Moral Sense, Analogy with Beauty, Sentimentalism versus Rationalism
