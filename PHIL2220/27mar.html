Hume’s Enquiry
Thu., Mar. 27
In this class, we finish our discussion of chapters 4–7 of Hume’s Enquiry concerning Human Understanding. These chapters contain Hume’s famous critique of causation. He argues that even the most sophisticated causal reasoning is ultimately based not on intellectual processes, but instead on the same sort of associative habituation by which animals learn from experience. The belief resulting from this process is nothing more than an idea held with a great deal of force and vivacity by the mind. And our idea of the ‘necessary connexion’ (by which a cause makes its effect happen) turns out to be nothing more than a strong feeling of expectation that we tend to project onto the external world.
Key Concepts: Copy Principle, Association, Personal Identity
