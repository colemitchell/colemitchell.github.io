Fourth, Fifth Meditation
Thu., Jan. 29
In this class, we cover the Fourth Meditation (“Truth and falsity”), and the Fifth Meditation (“The essence of material things, and the existence of God considered a second time”). In the Fourth Meditation, Descartes argues that we can always avoid intellectual error by restraining our will and refusing to make a judgment unless we have a clear and distinct perception. In the Fifth Meditation, he argues that material things have their own “true and immutable natures” that can intellectually perceived and used to demonstrate various properties, regardless of whether the material things really exist. He then provides a new demonstration of God’s existence, an ontological argument based on the fact that existence is a perfection and God has all perfections. Finally, he argues that “true and certain knowledge” (the kind that cannot ever be doubted) depends on a prior knowledge of God’s existence as a non-deceiver who guarantees the reliability of clear and distinct perception.
Key Concepts: Will, Intellect, Error; Essences, Ontological Argument, Cartesian Circle
