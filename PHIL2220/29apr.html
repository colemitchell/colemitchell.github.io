Ethics: self and others
Thu., Apr. 29
In this class, we discuss two ways in which a sentimentalist moral psychology can incorporate a sense of oneself in the eyes of others. For Jean-Jacques Rousseau, the “savage” humans who lived prior to the development of civilization, language, and reason enjoyed a simple psychology of self-preservation moderated by pity, without any social connections or concern for public esteem. But once we began to live together and make certain comparisons, the sentiment of amour-propre emerged, from which countless social/political ills ensued. For Adam Smith, our natural ability to sympathetically share in someone else’s feelings, which brings us great pleasure upon reaching an emotional harmony, forms the basis for our evaluation of the feelings of others and the feelings of oneself. When evaluating ourselves, Smith has us consulting the idea of an impartial spectator attempting to sympathize with our own feelings: this enables us to achieve a certain impartiality in our actions despite our very partial sentiments.
Key Concepts: Noble Savage, Amour de soi vs. Amour-propre, Sympathy, Impartial Spectator
