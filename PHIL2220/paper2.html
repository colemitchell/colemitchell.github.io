    • I have offered two different translations of the Critique of Pure Reason excerpt: the 1929 Kemp Smith translation and the 1998 Guyer-Wood translation (the version I originally gave you looked like this). Whichever one you have decided to use, you can cite its page number like this: “(Kant 1781: ###)”. For example:
      • According to Kant, the empirical world is completely deterministic, with no exceptions. Causal determinism is itself a law of nature, “from which no departure can be permitted, and from which no appearance may be exempted”. (Kant 1781: 470).

      • According to Kant, the empirical world is completely deterministic, with no exceptions. Causal determinism is itself a law of nature, “from which under no pretext can any departure be allowed or any appearance be exempted”. (Kant 1781: 538).
        [This student would be using the 1998 Guyer-Wood translation.]
    • I have offered only one translation of the Critique of Practical Reason excerpt: the 1996 Gregor translation. You can cite its page number like this: “(Kant 1788: ###)”. For example:
      • According to Kant, there is no easy way to reconcile free will and determinism. Those compatibilists who say that determinism poses no threat to free will as long as the determining grounds lie within the agent are guilty of a “wretched subterfuge”. (Kant 1788: 216).
