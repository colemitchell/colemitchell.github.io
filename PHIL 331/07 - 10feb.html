Huxley on epiphenomenalism
Mon., Feb. 10
This class session covers Thomas Huxley’s 1874 essay “On the Hypothesis that Animals are Automata, and its History”—a classic statement of epiphenome­nalism. Huxley proposes that animals and humans are automata: that our behavior and internal anatomical workings have purely physical, mechanical, non-mental causes. But this does not mean that humans or animals lack consciousness. Instead, Huxley suggests that the physical processes in humans and animals produce a (perhaps non-physical) consciousness as a mere byproduct or epiphenomenon—i.e., a side-effect that has no causal influence on anything, even our own behavior.
Key Concepts: Epiphenomenalism, Conscious Automata
