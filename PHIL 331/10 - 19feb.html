Identity-theory in the mid-century
Wed., Feb. 19
This class session covers the development of identity-theory materialism in the late ’50s by U. T. Place, Herbert Feigl, and J. J. C. Smart. Although Place, et al. were generally friendly to behaviorism, they proposed that consciousness/sensations/‘raw feels’ could perhaps be identified with certain internal states and processes: namely, brain states and processes. [Note: This view is sometimes referred to as ‘type-identity theory’, ‘the brain-process theory (or thesis)’, ‘central state materialism’, ‘type-physicalism’, or even just ‘physicalism’.]
Key Concepts: Contingent Identity, Sense and Reference
Readings:
Place, “Is Consciousness a Brain Process?” (Textbook ch. 8)
Smart, “Sensations and Brain Processes” (Textbook ch. 9)
(optional) Feigl, “The ‘Mental’ and the ‘Physical’” (Textbook ch. 10)
